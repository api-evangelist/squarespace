<p class="">Marketing <a href="https://www.squarespace.com/blog/how-to-start-event-planning-business" target="_blank">an events and experiences business</a> is different from marketing most services. Your work is visible before your clients ever meet you—people see the weddings you've styled, the corporate retreats you've pulled off, the milestone celebrations you've brought to life. That visibility is an asset, and the right marketing strategy helps you turn it into a steady stream of new clients.</p><p class="">Whether you plan <a href="https://www.squarespace.com/blog/wedding-website-examples" target="_blank">intimate weddings</a>, large-scale corporate conferences, brand activations, or private celebrations, the core challenge is the same. You’ll need to get the right people to find you, trust you, and book you. This guide walks you through the strategies that move the needle for events and experiences businesses, from building a brand that stands out to setting up systems that bring in repeat clients.</p>





















  
  




  
    
<section class="sq-key-takeaways"> 
  <h3 class="sq-key-takeaways__title" id="sq-key-takeaways-title"> 
    <span>Key takeaways 
  </h3> 

  <p> 
    Building a sustainable event-based business requires more than talent—it depends on how visible, credible, and accessible you are to potential clients. 
  </p> 

  <p> 
    The following principles highlight what consistently drives discovery, trust, and repeat bookings across competitive markets. 
  </p> 

  <ul class="sq-key-takeaways__list"> 
    <li><strong>Brand identity sets first impressions.</strong> A clear and consistent brand shapes how potential clients perceive your work before they ever make contact.</li> 
    <li><strong>Search visibility drives discovery.</strong> Strong presence on both traditional and AI-powered search platforms ensures you’re found when clients are actively looking.</li> 
    <li><strong>Social proof builds trust fastest.</strong> Reviews, testimonials, and word-of-mouth referrals carry more influence than almost any other marketing channel.</li> 
    <li><strong>Content builds long-term credibility.</strong> Educational content and an active blog keep your audience engaged and position you as an authority between bookings.</li> 
    <li><strong>Automation improves lead capture.</strong> Streamlined emails and contact forms help you capture and nurture inquiries without constant manual effort.</li> 
    <li><strong>Referrals create consistent growth.</strong> Strategic partnerships and referral programs can become two of your most reliable sources of new business.</li> 
  </ul> 
</section> 

 
<br /> 
  




  <h2>Establish an authentic brand identity</h2><p class="">Events and experiences businesses live and die by perception. A corporate event planner serving Fortune 500 companies projects something very different from a boutique wedding studio in a coastal town—and your brand needs to communicate that distinction instantly. Before someone reads a single word of your site copy, your visual identity is already doing the work.</p><p class="">Start by <a href="https://www.squarespace.com/blog/find-your-niche-service-business" target="_blank"><span>getting clear on your niche</span></a>—what you actually do best and who you do it for. Are you known for maximally personal micro-weddings, or for producing large-scale conferences with military-grade logistics? Do you specialize in immersive brand experiences, or in classic, elegant galas? That specificity is what makes your brand memorable and searchable. Generalist positioning tends to attract no one in particular.</p><p class="">Once you've defined your focus, translate it into a <a href="https://www.squarespace.com/blog/brand-visual-identity" target="_blank"><span>consistent visual identity</span></a>. Utilize this all over <a href="https://www.squarespace.com/blog/how-to-create-an-event-website" target="_blank">your website</a>: your color palette, typography, photography style, and logo. <a href="https://www.squarespace.com/logo" target="_blank"><span>Squarespace’s free logo generator</span></a> can help you create the latter. You’ll also want to develop a voice that carries through everything you publish. If you're building out your site copy, service descriptions, or client-facing content from scratch, <a href="https://www.squarespace.com/blog/ai-website-copy-generator" target="_blank"><span>Squarespace's AI writing tools</span></a> can give you a first draft you can revise until it matches your own voice.</p>

  <h2>Boost search visibility</h2><p class="">Most couples searching for a wedding planner and most HR managers looking for a corporate event production company start the same way: They search. Showing up in those results, especially at the top, can make the difference between a calendar that's full and one that's not. Investing in your search visibility is one of the highest-return things you can do for an events business.</p><p class=""><a href="https://www.squarespace.com/blog/squarespace-ai-seo-tools" target="_blank"><span>Squarespace's SEO and AEO tools</span></a> are built to help you optimize your pages for both traditional search engines and the AI-powered platforms people increasingly use to find <a href="https://www.squarespace.com/blog/local-ai-seo" target="_blank"><span>local services</span></a>. AEO, or <a href="https://www.squarespace.com/blog/ai-seo" target="_blank"><span>answer engine optimization</span></a>, is about making sure your site content is structured so AI tools like ChatGPT or Perplexity can surface it when someone asks a specific question—like "best corporate event planners in Chicago" or "how much does a wedding planner cost in Austin." Getting this right means showing up not just in Google, but in the answers those tools generate.</p><h3>Research and integrate keywords</h3><p class="">Think about the specific phrases a potential client would type when they're trying to find someone like you. "Wedding planner [city]," "corporate retreat planning company," "brand activation agency," and "luxury event design" are the kinds of search terms worth building into your site copy, page titles, and metadata. The goal is to weave them in where they belong—in your service descriptions, your about page, your location-specific landing pages—without forcing them into places they don't fit.</p><p class=""><a href="https://www.squarespace.com/blog/how-to-do-keyword-research" target="_blank"><span>Look at what your competitors rank for</span></a>, pay attention to the language clients use when they reach out to you, and build your keyword strategy around what you actually do and where you actually operate. Squarespace's SEO tools make it straightforward to update <a href="https://www.squarespace.com/blog/on-page-seo" target="_blank"><span>page titles, descriptions, and alt text</span></a> from one place.</p><h2>Utilize social proof</h2><p class="">People planning events are taking a significant leap of faith. They're handing over one of the most important days of their professional or personal life—and a meaningful amount of money—to someone they've likely only met a handful of times. <a href="https://www.squarespace.com/blog/guide-to-social-proof" target="_blank"><span>Reviews and testimonials</span></a> are what turn a warm lead into a signed contract. They answer the question every prospective client is really asking: "Can I trust this person with my celebration?"</p><p class="">For events-specific businesses, social proof is particularly powerful because it's specific. A testimonial that says "They transformed our company's annual gala into something our team will talk about for years" does some pretty powerful persuading for other corporate planners.</p><h3>Gather reviews and client testimonials</h3><p class="">The best time to ask for a review is right after an event, when the client is still in the glow of a great experience. Build the ask into your post-event follow-up process so it happens every time, not just when you remember to do it. A short, genuine message thanking them for the trust they placed in you and asking if they'd be willing to share their experience is usually all it takes.</p><p class=""><a href="https://www.squarespace.com/blog/client-testimonials-marketing-strategy" target="_blank">Display testimonials prominently on your website</a>: on your homepage, your services pages, and ideally near any booking or inquiry form. Pull quotes for social media, include them in proposal documents, and weave them into email campaigns when they're relevant. The more places a prospective client encounters evidence that your work delivers, the more confident they'll feel reaching out.</p><h2>Create educational or empowering content</h2><p class="">Event clients, particularly those planning a wedding or a major corporate function for the first time, arrive with a lot of questions and not a lot of certainty. Publishing content that answers those questions puts you in front of them early in their decision-making process, before they've even started comparing vendors. It also demonstrates that you know what you're talking about, which matters enormously in a trust-dependent industry.</p><p class="">Think about the content your ideal clients would find genuinely useful. A guide to corporate event planning timelines. A breakdown of what goes into a full-service wedding package versus a day-of coordination. A post on how to choose a venue for a company offsite. These pieces work for you long after you publish them.</p><h3>The power of the professional blog</h3><p class=""><a href="https://www.squarespace.com/blog/blogging-for-business" target="_blank"><span>A blog on your website</span></a> does several things at once: it improves your search visibility, gives potential clients a reason to stay on your site longer, and shows your expertise in a way a service page alone can't. It also gives you something substantive to share across your other marketing channels.</p><p class="">Posting consistently is often where events businesses fall short, especially in peak season when you're heads-down producing events. <a href="https://www.squarespace.com/blog/ai-website-copy-generator" target="_blank"><span>Squarespace's AI writing tools</span></a> can help you get a first draft on the page faster, so the blank-page problem doesn't slow you down. Start with the questions you get most often from clients, and turn those into posts.</p><h3>Newsletters as a marketing tool</h3><p class=""><a href="https://www.squarespace.com/blog/email-newsletter" target="_blank"><span>Email newsletters</span></a> let you stay in front of your audience between the moments when someone is actively searching for an event planner. For a wedding business, that might mean a monthly note covering planning tips, seasonal trends, and real weddings you've produced. For a corporate events business, it might mean a quarterly update on venue openings, event tech, or case studies from recent activations.</p><p class="">You can leverage <a href="https://www.squarespace.com/blog/ai-website-copy-generator" target="_blank"><span>Squarespace's AI writing tools</span></a> to draft and shape newsletter content, so you're not staring at a blank subject line after a long week on-site. Keep your newsletters focused on being useful—when someone is ready to book, you'll already be top of mind.</p>

  <h2>Personalize marketing efforts</h2><p class="">A bride planning a 60-person micro-wedding has very different needs and concerns than a head of employee engagement planning a 500-person offsite. Treating them the same in your marketing—sending identical emails, showing them the same featured work—is a missed opportunity. The more you can tailor your outreach to reflect what each type of client actually cares about, the more effective your marketing will be.</p><p class="">Start by <a href="https://www.squarespace.com/blog/what-is-customer-segmentation" target="_blank"><span>segmenting your audience</span></a>. At minimum, think about whether someone is a personal event client or a corporate one, whether they've already inquired or are earlier in the funnel, and whether they've worked with you before. From there, you can shape your messaging to speak to each group's specific priorities.</p><h3>Automate emails to engage and re-engage customers</h3><p class=""><a href="https://www.squarespace.com/blog/email-automation" target="_blank"><span>Automated emails</span></a> let you stay in touch at the moments that matter most: a welcome message when someone joins your list, a follow-up after an inquiry comes in, a check-in for clients who've gone quiet after an initial consultation. You can personalize these messages so they feel relevant rather than generic, referencing the type of event they're planning based on how they found you.</p><p class="">For events businesses, a few automated touchpoints are especially valuable, such as an immediate response when someone submits an inquiry form, <a href="https://www.squarespace.com/blog/drip-marketing-guide" target="_blank"><span>a nurture sequence</span></a> for leads who haven't yet booked, and a post-event message that thanks the client and gently invites a review or referral. These sequences do consistent work in the background so you can focus on the events themselves.</p><h2>Collect leads</h2><p class="">Traffic to your website is only valuable if you have a way to follow up with the people visiting it. Most events businesses attract potential clients who aren't ready to book on the first visit. They're in research mode, comparing options, figuring out what they need. If you have no way to capture their contact information, they'll leave and you'll never know they were there.</p><p class=""><a href="https://www.squarespace.com/blog/client-intake-form-examples" target="_blank">A contact form on your website</a> does double duty. It gives visitors an easy way to reach you and adds their information to your list so you can follow up. For events businesses, a well-designed inquiry form can also do a lot of pre-qualification work. Ask about event type, approximate guest count, preferred timeframe, and budget range. That information lets you respond with something tailored rather than generic, which immediately sets a better tone for the relationship.</p><p class="">You can also grow your list by offering something useful in exchange for an email address—a downloadable planning checklist, a guide to budgeting for a corporate offsite, a wedding timeline template. These resources attract the exact type of client you want to work with, and they give you a reason to follow up with genuinely helpful content.</p><h2>Develop thought leadership</h2><p class="">In the events industry, reputation is everything. When a venue coordinator gets asked for planner recommendations, or when a company's executive assistant is looking for an event production partner, the names that come up first are usually the ones people have seen and heard from. <a href="https://www.squarespace.com/blog/personal-branding" target="_blank"><span>Thought leadership</span></a> is how you become one of those names.</p><p class="">Think about where your ideal clients spend their time and what conversations they're already having. Corporate event planners might consider contributing articles to HR or operations publications, speaking at industry conferences, or appearing on podcasts about workplace culture and employee experience. Wedding planners might guest on wedding planning podcasts, contribute to editorial features in regional lifestyle publications, or speak at bridal expos. These appearances extend your reach to audiences who've never heard of you, and they carry a built-in credibility that paid advertising rarely matches.</p><p class="">Even within your own channels, you can build thought leadership through the specificity and depth of your content. Publishing a detailed breakdown of how you approached a complex multi-day corporate conference—the logistics, the vendor coordination, the contingency planning—shows prospective clients what working with you actually looks like.</p><h2>Lean into community-based marketing</h2><p class="">Events businesses are inherently tied to place. Even corporate event planners who work nationally tend to have a home market where most of their relationships live. And for wedding planners, venue relationships, local vendor networks, and regional reputation are core to the business model. Building presence in your local community—and in the online communities where your clients gather—pays dividends that are hard to replicate through digital advertising alone.</p><p class="">Consider sponsoring local bridal expos, business association events, or community festivals that align with your brand. Look for opportunities to participate in panels, workshops, or venue showcases. Join the online forums and groups where engaged couples or event professionals in your area connect, and contribute to those conversations in a way that's genuinely helpful rather than promotional.</p><p class="">For corporate event planners, joining professional associations and participating in industry groups for HR, operations, or executive assistants can put you in front of the people who actually hold the budget for events planning at their organizations.</p><h2>Create strategic partnerships</h2><p class="">The events industry runs on relationships. Venues, photographers, florists, caterers, audio-visual teams, travel coordinators, corporate gifting companies—every vendor in your network is a potential source of warm referrals, and you can be one for them in return. Strategic partnerships formalize that dynamic and give both parties a reason to keep sending business to each other.</p><p class="">Think about the businesses that serve the same clients you do but don't compete with you directly. A corporate event planner might build relationships with executive travel agencies, branded merchandise companies, or team-building facilitation firms. A wedding planner might develop preferred vendor relationships with photographers, florists, and luxury venues. When you refer clients to partners you trust, and they do the same for you, everyone's business gets stronger.</p><p class="">Make your partnerships visible. Mention your preferred vendors on your website, collaborate on content or social posts, and consider co-hosting workshops or styled shoots that showcase what you can do together. These collaborations expand your reach to each other's audiences and reinforce your reputation as a well-connected professional in your space.</p><h2>Bring it all together</h2><p class="">Marketing an events and experiences business takes consistency and a willingness to invest in relationships—with clients, with vendors, and with the broader community your work touches. The strategies in this guide build on each other. A strong brand brings the right people to your site, smart SEO and AEO tools help them find you, and great content and social proof turn visitors into clients. Keep showing up, keep refining what works, and the business you want to build will follow.</p>
